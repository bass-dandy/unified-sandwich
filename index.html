Structure

The arrangement of elements

An ideal sandwich has some number of solids and liquids held between two instances of a different solid. It is important to note that a sandwich's structure score has nothing to do with these elements themselves, merely their arrangement and state of matter. Take for example an aluminum composite panel, which consists of two thin aluminum sheets bonded to a non-aluminum core. Such an object scores similarly to a grilled cheese along this axis. More abstractly, sequences of events can have a high structural score when sampled over time. What is a day but a time of light between times of dark, a week but a time of labor between times of rest, and a life but one's experience between birth and death?

Composition

The parts in proportion

An ideal sandwich is, above all else, edible. It is composed mostly of bread, meat, cheese, lettuce, and some number of other vegetables and condiments, though alternative elements such as peanut butter and jelly are common. The composition score of a sandwich is based both on its component parts and the ratio between them. The search for the canonical set of ingredients and their golden ratio is ongoing, so scores along this axis tend to be more subjective than others (at least at the high end of the spectrum). Nonetheless it should be clear that inedibles always score lower than edibles regardless of their structure.

Geometry

The relative size and shape

An ideal sandwich can be held in one hand. It is approximately a rectangular cuboid in shape, with the opposing faces along the vertical axis possessing a convex hull that is nearly square and the distance between those faces being somewhat less than the length of their edges. Though the ideal shape is basically axiomatic, the ideal size is relative to the hand of the subject and therefore varies. A sandwich's geometry score is impacted by a its distance from these ideal measurements - the greater the difference, the lesser the score.

Temporality

The relative timing

An ideal sandwich exists concurrently with the subject. But what of the sandwich they just consumed? Though it is no longer a sandwich in the traditional sense, it is, in a way, still a sandwich, because it once was a sandwich. And what of the sandwich they are about to create? Though it is not yet a sandwich, it still has a high probability of becoming one. So it is that a not-yet sandwich must score the lowest in temporality, as there is no way to know for certain that it will ever come to be. The higher the probability of its sandwichhood the higher its score (a plate of bread, lettuce, tomato, and bacon is more likely to become a sandwich than would be a block of lead). A once-was sandwich scores relatively higher still, but ultimately both a sandwich in the future and a sandwich in the past are less of a sandwich than a sandwich in the present.

Intellection

The concept

An ideal sandwich exists in thought as well as matter. The very notion of a sandwich was born of human minds, thus without intellection a sandwich has no meaning. Indeed every sandwich was once but an idea - the idea of a BLT, the idea of a turkey club, the idea of a ham and swiss melt - their translation into matter nothing more than a detail of execution. It is not an exaggeration to say that without intellection there would be no way to score a sandwich at all, as there would no Platonic ideal against which a sandwich could be judged. While there are a multitude of ways to judge intellection in practice, in its most naive terms the idea of a sandwich is more of a sandwich than the idea of a bowl of spaghetti.
